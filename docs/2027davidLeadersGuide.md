## **Welcome to Camp Lawton, est. 1921**

When Catalina Council President James M Lawton declared that Tucson and Southern Arizona scouts needed a real permanent camp away from the heat of the desert, it's doubtful they anticipated that camp running continuously (with an asterix or two) for over 100 years. In 1921 the first campsite was established where the Palisades Ranger Station is now. In 1926, it moved to the current site. In 1947, utilizing the new road, they built many of the buildings you still see today. The BSA established the  National Camping Accreditation Program in 1967, and Camp Lawton has been fully accredited every year since (again, a few asterisks). 

But Accreditation is not the point. Camp Lawton exists for and because of *you*. Scouting America summer camps are designed to supplement your unit’s goals. We provide programs designed to help scouts advance in rank, earn merit badges, learn responsibility and leadership, and develop social skills. Camp Lawton’s scale allows us to customize the experience specifically to each scout's needs. That's something very special.

And yet there's also the other thing. Parents and leaders send their scouts to us for skill development and tangible progress; but the reason your *scouts* want to come is the FUN\! At Lawton, we know that all of our work is meaningless if the scouts aren't having a great time while they're doing it. A laughing scout is a leaning scout. That's why we make a point to create great campfires and evening experiences, fun campwide games, and a top notch staff that love what they do.

**II.  Arrival & Check-In**

**Getting to Camp**

Camp Lawton is the first camp on Organization Ridge road at Mile 20 of the Catalina Highway. If you pass the Palisades Ranger Station, you went too far. 

Getting to the Catalina Highway depends on where you’re coming from, but we live in an age of GPS on every phone, so I trust you can find it. If you’re coming from out of town, Tucson isn’t too bad to navigate on a Sunday, but you do need to cross town. The easiest route is to cross town via Grant, Speedway, or Broadway and make a left at Tanque Verde but in some cases staying on the freeway and coming up Houghton might be easier, putting you to the east of the Catalina Highway light.

Whatever the case, the road up the mountain is twisty and turny. It’s wide and completely safe provided you’re paying attention. Be cautious of cyclists on the road. There are no passing zones on the road, but the more courteous cyclists and drivers will pull into the many pullouts going up the road to let faster traffic by. Please be one of those courteous people yourself. If you are not comfortable keeping to the speed limit of 35, please pull off periodically to let faster traffic by.

**III.  Camp Policies**

**USFS Special Use Permit**

Camp Lawton operates on National Forest land under a USFS Special Use Permit, meaning USFS permit conditions legally prevail over camp preferences (NCAP AO-801). The following apply to all leaders, scouts, and visitors:

•   LEAVE NO TRACE: Pack out all trash. Stay on established trails. Do not trample native vegetation. Respect wildlife — observe from a distance, never feed.

•   CAMPFIRES: We are subject to USFS fire restrictions at all times. Violations can lead to significant fines or even criminal penalties. The fire danger on the mountain is nearly always “Very High” or “Extreme” on the mountain, meaning you should come to camp expecting not to have fires in your campsite at all. If conditions are favorable, the Camp Director will announce if restrictions are lifted. 

•   GROUND DISTURBANCE: There should be no digging, trenching, staking, or any other kind of ground disturbance without Camp Director and/or Ranger approval. The Forest Service retains oversight over any physical improvements or alterations to the camp, and that includes digging holes. Also, a side effect of being a century old camp, there are water and power lines all over the camp, often very shallow due to erosion over the years. 

•   PETS: No pets within camp boundaries without USFS approval. Service animals with current documentation are permitted. 

•   FIREARMS & FIREWORKS: Prohibited on National Forest land. No personal firearms may be brought to camp under *any* circumstances.

•   TRAIL USE: Group sizes on hiking trails may be restricted by your USFS permit. Confirm maximum group size for any off-road hike with the Camp Ranger before departure.

•   WASTE DISPOSAL: Dispose of all waste at designated central waste stations. Around camp are retired mail boxes installed as secure trash containers to prevent bear and other critter activity. Use them, but they must be emptied nightly. Never pour liquid waste on the ground.

•   WILDLIFE & FOOD: Store all food and scented items in the Bear Box as instructed when you arrive. Your campsite will have a designated box that should be stored overnight in the shed next to the Youth Shower House. Do not store valuables in these boxes as, unfortunately, a scout is *not always* Trustworthy. Report unusual wildlife activity to the Camp Ranger or any staff member immediately.

**Safeguarding Youth Guidelines**

* **All adults staying overnight or present at camp for 72 total hours or more must be registered** Scouting America members, and they must have a current **Safeguarding Youth Training (SYT)** certificate on file. The SYT program has officially replaced the retired Youth Protection Training (YPT) and must be renewed annually.  
* **Two registered adult leaders 21 years of age or older are required at all times**, and there must strictly be **no one-on-one contact between adults and youth**. Furthermore, if female youth or female adult program participants are present, **at least one registered female adult leader (21 or older) must be present**.  
* **Showers, latrines, and changing areas must be separated by both gender and age group (youth versus adults)**. If completely separate facilities are not available, **separate times must be scheduled and posted**. Adults must respect youth privacy and may only enter these areas in the event of an immediate health or safety emergency.  
* **Report any suspected child abuse immediately to local law enforcement and child protective services**, as all persons in Scouting are mandated reporters. After reporting to the authorities, you must also notify the **council Scout Executive** or call the 24-hour **Scouts First Helpline at 1-844-SCOUTS1**.  
* **The use of smartphones, cameras, drones, or any other recording devices is strictly prohibited in any area where privacy is expected**, such as restrooms, showers, or changing areas. To help enforce this, camps should locate device charging stations away from restroom and shower house facilities.

**Health & Safety**

•   First aid kits are located at: the Health Lodge, each program area, and carried by all trek leaders.

•   Use of the Buddy System (NCAP HS-511) is required for all scout movement throughout camp — brief your scouts at arrival.

**Emergencies**

In the event of a **campwide emergency**, the iron BELL on the side of the Dining Hall will begin ringing continuously and loudly. ALL persons in camp should report immediately to the Parade Grounds (the flag poles). Do not wait to walk up as a troop. We will do a full head count once gathered. Adults, staff, and leaders should ensure everyone in their vicinity gets to the Parade Grounds regardless of unit. If a scout is known missing, report it immediately to a member of the admin team. Once the head count has been completed, appropriate instructions will be given depending on the nature of the emergency. **Keep your car keys with you at all times in case an emergency evacuation becomes necessary.** 

The exception to the above would be an **active shooter** situation. This is an unlikely and terrifying situation, but we must have a plan for it. In that case, scouts are to hide in a program area or flee into the forest (carefully). Law enforcement will be called as soon as possible by staff, but if you are able to get a signal, please do so yourself. Only if absolutely necessary to protect human life should you attempt to fight the suspect. Once the incident is clear, the emergency bell will sound and we will do a head count as above. 


**Mountain Wildlife & Critters**

Situated within the Coronado National Forest, Camp Lawton is home to a diverse array of wildlife. While we are visitors in their habitat, a respectful distance ensures safety for all.

* **Spiders**: While harmless "Daddy Long Legs" and Jumping Spiders are common. Black Widows and Brown Recluces do occasionally nest in wood or rock piles. As a rule, they prefer to be left alone and will avoid areas with lots of human activity, but you may see them. Exercise caution.   
* **Snakes**: Rattlesnake sightings have increased in recent years. Maintain a safe distance and alert staff immediately if one is spotted near platforms or debris. Other snakes may be sighted as well. Enjoy and don’t touch..   
* **Squirrels**: Abert and Grey Squirrels are frequent guests in camp. Enjoy their presence from afar; do not approach or provide food. Report any erratic behavior.  
* **Bats**: These nocturnal residents are generally disease-free and harmless. As stewards of the forest, we must ensure the bat population remains undisturbed.  
* **Skunks**: Often seen in the early morning, these animals will not bother you if left alone. Avoid startling them to prevent their famous defense mechanism.  
* **Birds**: From hummingbirds and Stellar Jays to hawks and owls, Lawton offers exceptional birdwatching. Please observe our avian friends with respect.  
* **Turkeys**: Turkeys, yes the gobble gobble kind, live here and you might see them. They’re a marvel to see, especially if a male is performing for a female, but they are not harmless creatures. Stay back at least 20 feet to not provoke them. They have also been known to vandalize cars in the parking lot. Be careful not to give them reason to take interest in your vehicle.   
* **Bears**: While not seen in camp during the summer for many years, Black Bears do inhabit the mountain. They are generally cautious creatures who don’t want to be bothered. If encountered, stand tall and speak firmly to identify yourself as human. **Do not** run, climb trees, or play dead as that presents you as prey rather than an unnecessary hassle. If a bear cub is present, the mother is near; exit the area cautiously. Following "smellable" storage rules is mandatory to prevent luring them to your campsite. Report sightings to administration immediately.  
* **Mountain Lions**: While rare during camp season, sightings have occurred in spring and fall. If seen, speak loudly and retreat slowly. Report sightings to administration immediately.

**Natural Hazards & Mountain Weather**

* **Summer Storms**: Monsoons bring lightning, wind, and hail. Range activities stop at the first sound of thunder. We prioritize safety and if lightning gets close enough we may call an emergency and bring everyone to the Dining Hall as a shelter while attempting to continue programming as conditions allow.  
* **Forest Hazards**: Despite Forest Service mitigation, windy days increase the risk of falling branches. Protruding roots and rocks on trails are persistent tripping hazards due to erosion. Always utilize the buddy system and carry a flashlight at night.  
* **Fire Safety**: Wildfires are a critical threat. They have threatened the camp before and will again in the future, but we should note that we have never had even a minor casualty due to a forest fire.  Compliance with daily fire regulations is non-negotiable.

### **The Tribe of Papago**

The Tribe of Papago however continued as Camp Lawton’s recognition program. It has passed in and out of our memory over the years, but it’s time we start again, fresh. 

The primary role of the Tribe of Papago is recognition of Scouts returning to camp multiple years.

### The Barker Standard

Roy J. Barker (1924-2012) was a legendary scout leader. For decades he acted as a scoutmaster, Executive Board member, and Staff member. He was a mentor for all who knew him, and the troops he led were consistently shining examples of scout spirit. For that reason, we have named our honor troop award The Barker Standard. All troops who fulfill the requirements below will be recognized as winners of The Barker Standard and will receive a special Tribe of Papago rocker and a Ribbon for their troop flag. One troop, voted on by the staff on Friday night, will be specially recognized with space on a plaque in the dining hall for the remainder of the summer.   
   
In order to receive the award, a troop must:

1. Request and pass a Uniform Inspection by a Senior Staff member  
2. Request and pass a Campsite Inspection by a Senior Staff member  
3. Lead a Flag Ceremony   
4. Sing a Song at a camp wide activity   
5. Perform a skit, song, or story at the campwide closing campfire.  
6. Volunteer for cleanup duty in the Mess Hall and/or the shower facilities.  
7. Complete a service project while in camp.  
8. Demonstrate consistent Scout Spirit, Live the Scout Oath, Law, Motto, Slogan, and Outdoor Code throughout the week.  
9. Request and complete the Barker Standard application form

**FAQ**

**How many merit badges can a scout earn in a week?** 

That depends on how much the scout likes their free time… and sanity. Technically there are 6 sessions in a day. A scout willing to face the gauntlett and some clever scheduling could probably sign them up for 6 badges. We don’t recommend that though. That is a very heavy load for a week of what’s supposed to be fun. That’s not to say we want your scouts running around bored with nothing to do, but Camp Lawton offers more than just little round patches.

**How big is the camp?**

Camp Lawton is roughly 60 acres. That’s about 45 football fields, 0.1 square miles. That’s not much, but with the thinner air, it feels like more when you’re walking across it. We believe our small size is an advantage. It allows us to pack in so much more intimate of an experience for each scout and giving each scout a chance to get to know our staff personally as role models and leaders. 

**Why no Aquatics program?**

Our pool, which we kept functioning until 2024 was a relic of a bygone time even 50 years ago. We finally reached the point where we had to take it to a farm upstate where it will live forever frollicking with other tiny, freezing swimming pools just like it. Realistically, it just became more trouble than it was worth. Will we never have a pool again? Never say never. An extremely generous donation and an equally generous approval from the Forest Service for the major construction could certainly get us back in the Aquatics Program game. 

**Why the BB Guns?** 

Technically they’re Airsoft Rifles, but yeah. Many camps have 22s. We however are in a relatively congested area on leased National Forest land. The decision was made back in the 90s that the sound and environmental impact of the bullets in the backstop was just not sustainable and we were told to stop. In order to keep our Rifle program, we were able to switch to low power rifles and now everyone is happy. In fact, quite a few scouts have found it a better experience not having to deal with the noise, recoil, and ammo fees. 

**What if something goes wrong and I can’t register my scouts for all their merit badges online?**

Don’t worry about it. Contact us in advance if you can but when you arrive at camp, we’ll do our best to correct the issue or find a good alternative. We won’t leave any scout out in the cold.

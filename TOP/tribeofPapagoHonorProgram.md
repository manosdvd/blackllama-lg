# **The Tribe of Papago: History, Symbolism, and Relevance of the Camp Lawton Honor Program (1923–Present)**

## **Introduction to the Era of Camp Fraternalism**

In the years immediately following the First World War, the American youth camping movement underwent a period of explosive growth, fundamentally altering the landscape of outdoor education. Driven by a national imperative to instill civic virtue, physical hardiness, and moral fortitude in young men, organizations such as the Boy Scouts of America (BSA) rapidly expanded their summer camp infrastructure. However, during this nascent era of the 1910s and 1920s, the national BSA apparatus operated with a high degree of decentralization. Local councils, camp directors, and regional executives were granted broad autonomy to develop unique, localized programmatic solutions to the challenges of camp management. Foremost among these challenges were the retention of older, experienced campers and the acquisition of a reliable volunteer labor force to construct and maintain primitive camp infrastructure.  
It was within this crucible of decentralized innovation and localized fraternalism that the "Tribe of Papago" was forged in 1923 at Camp Lawton, a remote Boy Scout facility situated high in the Santa Catalina Mountains outside of Tucson, Arizona1. Conceived by Camp Director and Scout Executive Harry Ogle, the Tribe of Papago was an intricately designed honor society that rewarded returning campers for their loyalty, leadership, and physical exertion in service to the camp2.  
The historical trajectory of the Tribe of Papago is a profound anomaly within the broader history of the Boy Scouts of America. During the 1920s, nearly one hundred localized camp honor societies emerged across the United States2. The vast majority of these esoteric orders were eventually disbanded or entirely absorbed by the BSA's official national honor society, the Order of the Arrow (OA), following a national standardization mandate in 19483. The Tribe of Papago, however, survived this institutional purge. Through a brilliant administrative bifurcation, the Catalina Council fostered the creation of a namesake OA lodge—Papago Lodge 494—while simultaneously redefining the original Tribe of Papago as a conservation and alumni society, allowing both traditions to coexist into the twenty-first century2.  
This report provides an exhaustive sociological, historical, and symbolic analysis of the Tribe of Papago. It explores the foundational history of Camp Lawton, the structural architecture of Harry Ogle’s honor program, the evolution of its material culture, and the intricate, deeply respectful relationship it forged with the indigenous Tohono O'odham Nation. To ensure absolute academic clarity, it must be explicitly stated that the "Tribe of Papago" discussed herein is a fraternal youth organization and must not be confused with the actual indigenous Tohono O'odham people (historically known to Spanish settlers as the Papago), nor should Camp Lawton in Arizona be confused with the American Civil War prison site in Georgia of the same name, where over 10,000 Union prisoners were held in 18642.

## **The Geographic and Historical Crucible of Camp Lawton**

To fully comprehend the operational necessity and enduring relevance of the Tribe of Papago, one must first analyze the severe environmental and economic realities that characterized early Scouting in Southern Arizona. The unforgiving climate of the Sonoran Desert dictated the terms of outdoor recreation; summer camping in the valley floor was logistically perilous and profoundly uncomfortable. Consequently, the leadership of the local scouting apparatus recognized the urgent need for a high-altitude alpine refuge.

### **Early Encampments and the Push for a Permanent Site**

Prior to 1921, the Boy Scouts of the Tucson area—organized under what would eventually become the Catalina Council—utilized the old, abandoned ruins of Fort Lowell for their campouts, target practice, and father-and-son day outings8. While suitable for weekend excursions, Fort Lowell could not sustain a long-term residential summer camp. The search for a permanent facility became the premier civic project of the newly formed Rotary Club of Tucson9.  
A dedicated site selection committee was assembled, heavily populated by Rotary members, and chaired by James M. Lawton, who was concurrently serving as the president of the Catalina Council8. Prominent committee members included John B. Lyman Jr., who drafted the initial building plans; Kirke T. Moore, the chair of the Camping Committee; and Leroy C. "Jesse" James, who was tasked with the formidable challenge of securing vehicles capable of transporting boys up the rugged mountain terrain9. While the federal government had offered a ten-acre parcel in White House Canyon (Madera Canyon) in the Santa Rita Mountains, the committee ultimately favored a location on the south slope of Mount Bigelow, situated on Mount Lemmon in the Santa Catalina Mountains10. By June 1921, the site was officially selected near the junction of two mountain trails9. In honor of the council president who spearheaded the initiative, the facility was named Camp Lawton8.

### **The Primitive Realities of the 1920s Camp Experience**

Financed by an initial fundraising effort that amassed merely $400, Camp Lawton opened its inaugural season in 1921, hosting 68 boys over two separate two-week sessions9. The logistical execution of this early camp was an exercise in extreme frontier endurance. The modern, paved Catalina Highway did not exist. To reach the camp, supplies and campers had to be transported via the "Control Road," a treacherous, one-way, seven-mile dirt track leading from Oracle to Summerhaven10.  
The camp's infrastructure was practically non-existent in its first few years. Boys slept in surplus Army tents, drank rehydrated KLIM powdered milk, and bathed in the freezing waters downstream from the camp10. The transportation of heavy canvas, tools, and food required the deployment of old Army trucks and burro trains borrowed from the San Xavier mission10. This reliance on pack animals persisted until 1933, when federal prisoners from Prison Camp No. 10 were utilized to construct a connecting road to the scout camp10.  
Operating in this austere environment meant that maintenance and construction could not be outsourced to professional contractors; the budget simply did not allow for it. The physical survival and expansion of Camp Lawton depended entirely on the free, intensive manual labor of the Scouts and their adult leaders. Trail clearing, latrine digging, and structural repairs had to be executed by the campers themselves1. This operational imperative set the stage for the creation of a sociological mechanism designed to extract and reward this labor: the Tribe of Papago.

## **Harry Ogle and the Architecture of the Tribe of Papago**

In 1923, Harry Ogle, serving dual roles as the Camp Director of Camp Lawton and the Scout Executive for the council, recognized a critical administrative vulnerability2. The camp needed a reliable cadre of older, experienced boys to return year after year. These older youth were essential not only to provide peer leadership for younger, inexperienced Tenderfoot Scouts but also to execute the heavy physical labor required to sustain the camp's infrastructure. Ogle needed a system that transformed camp chores from a resented obligation into a highly coveted privilege.

### **The Gamification of Conservation and Labor**

Drawing heavy inspiration from the era's fascination with romanticized Native American lore—a pedagogical tool popularized by early Scouting pioneers such as Ernest Thompson Seton—Ogle authored the ceremonies, rituals, and advancement criteria for a new honor society2. He named this society the "Tribe of Papago," paying homage to the indigenous inhabitants of the surrounding Sonoran Desert2.  
Ogle's genius lay in the gamification of conservation and camp service. By framing physical labor as a sacred "task" required for elevation within a secret, mystical brotherhood, he effectively guaranteed a motivated workforce1. The Tribe was explicitly dedicated to brotherhood, service to the camp, and the encouragement of active participation in the camp's programmatic offerings1. Members were taught to develop a profound, almost spiritual, personal relationship with the specific geography of Camp Lawton1.

### **The Progressive Hierarchy**

The structural architecture of the Tribe of Papago was rigidly stratified, appealing to the adolescent psychological desire for status, recognition, and progression. Upon embarking on the quest to join the Tribe, a Scout was required to fulfill a highly specific series of tasks that incrementally increased in difficulty and time commitment over a period of four years1.  
The youth hierarchy was divided into four distinct ranks: Hunter, Warrior, Medicine Man, and Chief2. The integration of physical exertion (hiking specific camp trails like the Eagle Trail or Mount Bigelow), tangible productivity (earning merit badges), and spiritual reverence (attending chapel services) ensured that the Tribe functioned as a holistic educational matrix1.

### **The Integration of Adult Mentorship**

A successful youth organization requires institutional memory and consistent adult supervision. Recognizing this, Ogle built adult integration directly into the fabric of the Tribe. Adult leaders, or youth campers who had reached their fifth year at camp but did not hold the rank of Chief, were granted the title of **Elder**1. The requirements for an Elder were strictly codified: they were mandated to encourage and support younger Scouts in earning their tribal ranks, participate in four hours of service projects, hike two trails, volunteer in a program area for at least one hour, and attend a spiritual service1.  
Furthermore, reflecting the terminology and gender integration of camp staff in the 1920s, adult women who facilitated the camp's operations—such as camp nurses, administrative staff, or cooks—were historically inducted under the title of **Squaw**2. While contemporary perspectives on indigenous nomenclature have rightly evolved, the historical inclusion of female staff highlights the all-encompassing, communal nature of the early Camp Lawton experience. The Tribe of Papago was not merely a club for boys; it was the structural glue that held the entire camp community together.

## **Material Culture, Symbolism, and Ceremonial Mysticism**

The psychological efficacy of the Tribe of Papago relied heavily on public recognition of status, underpinned by esoteric symbolism and ceremonial mysticism. A Scout who successfully completed his progressive tasks was formally invited to an induction ceremony1. These ceremonies, strictly adhering to the rituals authored by Harry Ogle, were traditionally held on Friday nights following the camp-wide closing campfire1.  
In a notable departure from the strict secrecy maintained by many collegiate and adult fraternal lodges of the era, the Tribe of Papago's Friday night ceremony was open to family and friends1. This open-door policy functioned both as a mystical induction rite for the youth and as a highly visible public relations tool, showcasing the Scouts' dedication to their parents and reinforcing the value of the Scouting program within the Tucson community.

### **The Artifacts of the Tribe: Beanies and Medals**

The identification of rank is a critical component of any honor society. While the Order of the Arrow later standardized the use of the white sash adorned with a red arrow, localized camp societies in the 1920s and 1930s experimented with a wide array of regalia, including pins, belt awards, and felt patches5.  
Archival research, particularly the collections documented by Scouting historians such as Bill Topkis, confirms the existence of highly specific material artifacts associated with the early Tribe of Papago. Primary among these artifacts are the **Tribe of Papago Beanie** and the **1932 Tribe of Lawton Medal**13.  
The use of felt beanies (often referred to as "skulcaps") was a ubiquitous phenomenon in early 20th-century youth organizations. A comparative analysis of similar non-OA camp societies of the era reveals how these artifacts functioned. For instance, the Tribe of Tahoe, operating in the Golden Empire Council, explicitly color-coded its beanies to denote rank: a blue beanie for a Brave, red for a Warrior, gold for a Medicine Man, and white for a Chief14. Another group, the Clan of the Mystic Oak (CMO), utilized a six-panel green and gold beanie with felt lettering that changed colors based on the member's degree15.  
Given the widespread standardization of this practice across the American West, it is highly probable that the Tribe of Papago Beanie served an identical function at Camp Lawton—acting as an immediate, visual indicator of a camper's status and labor investment within the Tribe13. The existence of the 1932 Tribe of Lawton Medal further indicates that formal, metallic decorations were commissioned by the council to reward exceptional achievement, likely reserved for those who attained the rank of Chief or who contributed extraordinary service to the Catalina Council13.

## **The National Context: The Rise of the Order of the Arrow**

To fully appreciate the uniqueness of the Tribe of Papago's survival, it must be contextualized within the broader macro-environment of BSA internal politics and the aggressive nationalization of the Order of the Arrow.

### **The Proliferation of Rival Fraternities**

When Harry Ogle founded the Tribe of Papago in 1923, he was participating in a massive, uncoordinated national trend. By the mid-1920s, there were nearly one hundred distinct, localized summer camp honor societies operating autonomously across the United States2. The landscape of American scouting was a patchwork quilt of competing fraternal orders. Prominent examples included the Tribe of Tahquitz in California, the Tribe of Siwanis in Nevada, the Tribe of Mic-O-Say in Missouri and Kansas, Firecrafters in Indiana, the Clan of the Mystic Oak in the National Capital Area, and the Tribe of Gimogash4.  
Concurrently, a different honor society was beginning to gain national traction. Founded in 1915 by Dr. E. Urner Goodman and Carroll A. Edson at the Treasure Island Camp in the Philadelphia Council, this group was originally known by the secret Lenape phrase *Wimachtendienk Wingolauchsik Witahemui* (WWW)3. At the 1922 Scout Executives Conference, the BSA recognized WWW, alongside the Tribe of Gimogash and Ku-Ni-Eh, as "official experiments," formally permitting these societies to operate5. To avoid public confusion with the Industrial Workers of the World (IWW), WWW officially adopted the public name "Order of the Arrow" (OA) in 19245.

### **The Dark Side of Decentralization and the 1948 Mandate**

As the decades progressed, the BSA National Council grew increasingly alarmed by the profound lack of standardized values among the myriad local camp societies. E. Urner Goodman’s Order of the Arrow was strictly predicated on democratic principles—most importantly, the rule that non-members must elect the members, ensuring the society remained a true reflection of peer-recognized honor rather than an exclusive clique4.  
Conversely, some localized societies had devolved into toxic, exclusionary fraternities. Certain groups utilized highly secretive blackballing methods to deny entry to "undesirables," a practice frequently weaponized to enforce racial segregation or religious exclusion4. One notorious example was the Pathfinders of the Golden Trail (PGT), operating in the Jacksonville Council in Florida, which explicitly incorporated white supremacist principles into its secret bylaws4.  
To eradicate these abhorrent practices and unify the ideological framework of its honor programs, the Boy Scouts of America embraced the Order of the Arrow as an official program in 1934, and ultimately elevated it to the singular, official national honor society of the BSA in 19483. This 1948 mandate was an institutional death warrant for the localized camp societies. The BSA ordered that almost all independent fraternities either disband entirely or assimilate into the OA by converting their membership rolls into OA lodges4.

The implementation of the 1948 mandate resulted in the rapid extinction of most local traditions. The following table illustrates the diverse fates of several prominent pre-OA societies, highlighting the highly unusual outcome achieved by the Catalina Council.

## **The Bifurcation Strategy: Birth of Papago Lodge 494 and Survival of the Tribe**

The wave of OA standardization arrived in Tucson in 1948, carried by a man named Norman R. Horwitz2. Horwitz was a highly experienced Arrowman who had relocated to Arizona from Chicago, where he had achieved the prestigious Vigil Honor in 1938 as a member of Owasippi Lodge \#72. Recognizing that the Catalina Council lacked a chapter of the national honor society, Horwitz approached Scout Executive Merle Kruiish in 1952 to formally propose the creation of an Order of the Arrow lodge2.  
A formidable committee was immediately established to execute this transition. Chaired by Horwitz, with Kruiish serving as the official Adviser, the committee was rounded out by prominent local Scouting figures: Clinton Helbig, LeBaron Jones, Edward Ronstadt, and the legendary Otis Chidester2. Otis H. Chidester was an absolute titan of Southern Arizona Scouting; born in 1903, he served continuously in the BSA for over 84 years and was deeply committed to preserving the history of the movement2.  
The committee successfully petitioned the National Council, and on May 29, 1953, a national charter was officially granted to **Papago Lodge 494**2. Operating as the inaugural Lodge Chief, Norman Horwitz single-handedly conducted the 1953 induction ceremonies, bringing eighty-four new Arrowmen into the fold through the Lodge's first Ordeal2. Following this success, youth elections were held, and David Zinder was elected as the first youth Lodge Chief2.

### **The Semantic Pivot: How the Tribe Survived**

In almost every other BSA council across the nation, the chartering of a new OA lodge necessitated the immediate termination of the pre-existing camp society4. However, the leadership of the Catalina Council executed a brilliant administrative and semantic maneuver. Rather than allowing the Order of the Arrow to consume and erase the legacy of Harry Ogle's program, the camp leadership bifurcated the two entities.  
The newly chartered OA organization took the name "Papago Lodge 494," honoring both the local indigenous people and the historical legacy of the 1923 camp society3. Concurrently, the original "Tribe of Papago" was legally and functionally redefined. To comply with the strict national mandate that the OA must be the *only* official "honor society," the Tribe of Papago stripped away its elective, exclusionary status. It was rebranded as a "conservation group" and alumni association that was open to *all* campers4.  
Because the Tribe's advancement requirements (Hunter, Warrior, Medicine Man, Chief) were predicated entirely on tangible metrics—service hours, merit badge completion, and trail hiking—rather than peer elections, it could be legitimately classified as a programmatic advancement activity rather than a competing fraternal order1. This pivot saved the Tribe. While Papago Lodge 494 handled the esoteric, election-based functions dictated by national policy, the Tribe of Papago continued to operate at Camp Lawton as an organic, merit-based work corps, preserving its primary function of maintaining the physical infrastructure of Mount Lemmon1.

### **The Sioux System Disruption**

While the Tribe of Papago successfully navigated the national politics of the 1940s and 1950s, organizational continuity in localized camp programs is invariably subject to the whims of prevailing administrative leadership. In the late 1960s, the Tribe experienced a significant, albeit temporary, disruption when a new administrative paradigm known as the "Sioux System" was implemented at Camp Lawton2.  
The "Sioux System" was an imported organizational structure, likely brought in by an outside camp director seeking to modernize or refresh the camp's thematic programming2. However, imported, artificial systems rarely resonate with deeply entrenched alumni bases. The Sioux System effectively displaced the Tribe of Papago for a few years, but it ultimately failed to generate the same level of loyalty and volunteer labor2. By the early 1970s, the Sioux System was abandoned, and the historic Tribe of Papago was triumphantly reinstated as the premier conservation tradition of Camp Lawton2.

## **Etymology, Symbolism, and the Tohono O'odham Nation**

The etymology and cultural iconography of both the Tribe of Papago and Papago Lodge 494 present a fascinating case study in the evolving relationship between the Boy Scouts of America and Native American cultural identity.

### **The Evolution of the "Papago" Nomenclature**

When Harry Ogle named the society in 1923, and when the OA Lodge adopted the moniker in 1953, the term "Papago" was the universally recognized title for the indigenous peoples inhabiting the Sonoran Desert of southern Arizona and northwestern Mexico2. Historically, "Papago" was an exonym assigned to the Nation by Spanish conquistadors and missionaries, translating roughly to "bean eaters"2.  
For over six decades, the Catalina Council operated under this nomenclature, viewing it as a respectful homage to the region's original inhabitants. However, the socio-cultural landscape shifted in 1986 when the Native American nation officially rejected the Spanish exonym and legally reverted to its traditional endonym: the **Tohono O'odham** Nation, meaning "Desert People"2.  
During the late 20th century, many youth organizations faced mounting, justifiable pressure to abandon Native American names and mascots due to concerns regarding cultural appropriation. Demonstrating profound respect for this cultural shift, the leadership of Papago Lodge approached the Tohono O'odham Nation to discuss the logistics of rebranding the lodge. In a remarkable demonstration of mutual respect, the leadership of the Tohono O'odham Nation explicitly requested that the OA Lodge *not* change its name2. Because the Scouts had utilized the name with reverence, and because the historical legacy of the Lodge was so deeply intertwined with community service, the Nation blessed the continuation of the title. As a result, the name "Papago" remains permanently affixed to both the Lodge and the Camp Lawton conservation tribe2.

### **The Evolution of the Totem: From Flying Bird to Papago Pete**

While the name of the organization remained constant, the visual and performative representations of the society evolved dramatically over the decades, reflecting a continuous search for authentic Southwestern symbolism.  
When Papago Lodge 494 was founded in 1953, its original totem was designated as an "Arrowhead inscribed with a Flying Bird"2. Curiously, this initial concept was entirely theoretical and never materialized on any physical patches, neckerchiefs, or insignia2.  
In May 1957, heavily influenced by Norman Horwitz, the totem was officially changed to a steer skull entwined with a rattlesnake forming the number "20"2. This rugged imagery was thought to commemorate the twentieth anniversary of Horwitz's elevation to the Vigil Honor, and it became the first design to grace a Lodge pocket flap patch2.  
By 1961, the Lodge shifted its aesthetic alignment once again, seeking a symbol more deeply tied to local archaeology. The totem was changed to a Hohokam-inspired conception of the Gambel's quail2. The resilience of this symbol was tested in 1964 when the Papago Lodge absorbed the Victorio Lodge of the Cochise Council2. Victorio Lodge, named after the famous Chiricahua Apache warrior Bidu-ya, utilized the wild turkey as its totem2. When the lodges merged, the combined membership voted to retain the Gambel's quail, cementing its status2.  
This iconic bird, affectionately nicknamed "Papago Pete," became the enduring, beloved symbol of the organization2. In the 1980s, Arrowman Jeff Babis crafted a full-sized "Papago Pete" mascot costume, placing Papago Lodge among the first in the nation to employ a physical mascot2. When this original costume was finally retired during the Lodge's 60th Anniversary in 2013, it was placed on permanent loan to the Otis H. Chidester Scout Museum in Tucson, preserving it as a vital piece of scouting history2.

### **The Evolution of Ceremonial Regalia**

The performative aspect of the induction ceremonies also underwent significant historical revisions, reflecting a growing desire for anthropological accuracy. Initially, the Lodge utilized Hopi-inspired regalia for its induction rites. However, in 1986, seeking deeper historical resonance with the pre-Columbian cultures of Southern Arizona, the Lodge officially changed its cultural affiliation to the Hohokam—a group recognized as having split from the pre-Aztec Toltec civilization2.  
Consequently, Papago Ceremonialists began performing in elaborate Toltec-style regalia, adorned with brightly colored plumage and golden jewelry, a stark departure from the typical aesthetics of North American scouting lodges2. In more recent years, practical considerations regarding the maintenance and construction of such elaborate outfits have led the Lodge to adopt standard Plains-style regalia for its induction ceremonies2. The Lodge's dedication to performative excellence was nationally recognized; from 1957 to 1961, Papago members presided over the first five Area Conferences, and in 1958, 1966, and 1988, their dance and ceremony teams were ranked among the most outstanding in the nation2.

## **Archival Preservation and the Legacy of Otis Chidester**

The rich history of the Tribe of Papago, Papago Lodge 494, and Camp Lawton would likely have been lost to time were it not for the extraordinary dedication of Otis H. Chidester. As a founding member of the 1952 Papago Lodge committee, Chidester recognized early on the importance of preserving the material and documentary history of the Boy Scouts of America2.  
Chidester's involvement in Scouting spanned an incredible 84.5 years, making him the nation's senior scout in continuously registered active service at the time of his death in 199718. In 1984, he established the Scout Museum of Southern Arizona (later renamed in his honor), converting his own home on E. Blacklidge Drive in Tucson into a massive repository for Scouting artifacts18. The museum actively collects, preserves, and exhibits written, photographic, and physical records relating to the BSA, with a specific mandate to preserve the history of Southern Arizona18. By housing artifacts such as the original "Papago Pete" costume, early merit badge pamphlets from the 1920s, and the foundational documents of Camp Lawton, Chidester ensured that the legacy of Harry Ogle's 1923 experiment would be available for future generations of academic and historical researchers2.

## **Conclusion**

The "Tribe of Papago" represents an extraordinary, highly resilient artifact in the history of American youth organizations. Conceived in 1923 by Harry Ogle at Camp Lawton, the program was fundamentally an economic and sociological masterstroke engineered to secure free labor for primitive camp maintenance, drive camper retention, and build an unbreakable emotional tether between young men and the physical environment of Mount Lemmon.  
While hundreds of similar camp fraternities across the United States were eradicated by the aggressive nationalization of the Order of the Arrow in 1948, the leadership of the Catalina Council demonstrated remarkable administrative agility. By birthing the Papago Lodge 494 to fulfill the esoteric and elective requirements of the national BSA, and simultaneously rebranding the original Tribe of Papago as a merit-based conservation and alumni group, the Council ensured the survival of its localized heritage.  
The history of the Tribe of Papago—and its sister organization, Papago Lodge 494—highlights the complex, continually evolving dynamic between early Scouting and Native American culture. From the transition of Hopi to Toltec regalia, to the unique, highly respectful endorsement by the Tohono O'odham Nation to preserve the "Papago" moniker, the organization reflects a constant negotiation between historical tradition, cultural respect, and institutional identity.  
Today, the Tribe of Papago continues to fulfill Harry Ogle's original vision. Having provided individual recognition to over 5,000 honor campers, it remains a vital mechanism that transforms adolescent enthusiasm into tangible camp improvements2. As Scouts at Camp Lawton continue to hike the Eagle Trail, log conservation service hours, and attend Friday night campfires, they are participating in a century-old continuum, irrevocably bound together by a shared dedication to the high-altitude refuge of Mount Lemmon.

## Critical Information — Read Before You Make Plans

- **No aquatics:** Camp Lawton has no pool or waterfront. There is no swimming, boating, or other aquatics program.
- **Week 1 is shorter:** June 1–5 is a Tuesday-arrival, five-day session. Weeks 2 and 3 begin Sunday.
- **National Forest land:** Camp operates under a United States Forest Service special-use permit. Leave No Trace practices and all posted land-use restrictions are mandatory.
- **Fire restrictions:** Campfires may be prohibited at any time. Come prepared for a no-flame week and check with staff daily.
- **Vehicles:** Unauthorized vehicles are not permitted beyond the parking area. All gear must be carried to the campsite. Back into your parking space and keep emergency access clear.
- **Range program:** Archery is planned. Air-rifle programming depends on approved equipment, the permit, and required qualified staffing. Confirm availability before your session.
- **Medical forms:** Bring a current BSA Annual Health and Medical Record, Parts A, B, and C, for every participant.
- **Medications:** Bring prescription medications in original labeled containers. Turn them over to the Camp Health Officer at check-in for secure storage and administration.
- **Dietary and accommodation needs:** Submit them as early as possible so the camp can plan effectively.
- **Safeguarding youth:** Every adult must meet current Scouting America registration and training requirements. Two-deep leadership and the prohibition on one-on-one adult/youth contact apply at all times.

# I. Welcome to Camp Lawton

Camp Lawton was established in 1921 after Catalina Council leaders recognized that Southern Arizona Scouts needed a permanent mountain camp away from the desert heat. The original camp stood near today’s Palisades Ranger Station. It moved to its present site in 1926, and many of the historic buildings still in use were constructed beginning in the late 1940s.

Accreditation, policies, and facilities matter, but they are not the point of camp. Camp Lawton exists to support the goals of your unit and the growth of each Scout. The program is designed to help young people advance in rank, earn merit badges, practice responsibility and leadership, build friendships, and become more capable outdoors.

Camp must also be fun. Scouts may arrive thinking about badges and requirements, but the memories that bring them back are often the songs, campfires, games, challenges, staff members, and unexpected moments between scheduled activities. The 2027 program is built around both achievement and adventure.

## Pirate Crew Adventures

Welcome aboard. For 2027, Camp Lawton becomes a fleet of pirate ships. Every campsite is a Ship, every unit is a Pirate Crew, and every Scout has a chance to earn their sea legs.

The theme draws from the adventurous legends of the Golden Age of Piracy, with an emphasis on teamwork, navigation, outdoor skill, service, and treasure earned through achievement. All programming is land-based.

Please read this guide completely and share the relevant portions with Scouts, parents, guardians, and other unit leaders before departure.

# II. Before You Arrive

## Unit Leadership and Registration

Your unit must arrive with the adult leadership required by current Scouting America policy.

- Maintain two registered adult leaders age 21 or older with the unit.
- When female youth are present, provide the required registered female adult leader age 21 or older.
- Confirm that every adult has completed the current safeguarding-youth training required by Scouting America.
- Make sure every youth and adult is properly registered for the activity before arrival.
- Maintain two-deep leadership throughout travel, camp, and all unit activities.

## Required Documentation

Bring the following for every participant:

- BSA Annual Health and Medical Record Parts A, B, and C, completed within the required period
- Signed unit roster with emergency contact information
- Documentation for dietary needs, allergies, medical accommodations, or accessibility needs
- Prescription medications in original labeled containers with current dosage instructions
- Proof of current safeguarding-youth training for registered adult leaders
- Any additional forms requested in the final registration packet

Organize forms by participant before arrival. A clean packet will significantly speed up check-in.

## Electronic Health Records

Camp Lawton is developing electronic health-record capability under NCAP HS-503.

- Paper health forms remain acceptable for 2027.
- When submitting records electronically before camp, confirm that camp leadership received them.
- Health information is confidential and is available only to authorized health and emergency personnel.

## Dietary Needs and Medical Accommodations

Send dietary restrictions and accommodation requests as early as possible. The kitchen and health staff can solve far more problems with advance notice than they can at the serving line.

Include enough detail to distinguish between:

- Food allergy
- Medical restriction
- Religious or ethical dietary practice
- Food intolerance
- Personal preference

Families should discuss serious allergies, rescue medication, mobility needs, behavioral supports, and other substantial concerns directly with camp leadership before arrival.

## Packing for Camp Lawton

Camp Lawton sits at high elevation. Days may be warm, nights may be cold, and summer storms can arrive quickly.

Every participant should bring:

- Warm sleeping bag and insulated sleeping pad
- Rain gear
- Closed-toe shoes suitable for rocky trails
- Warm layer for evenings
- Sun protection and refillable water bottle
- Flashlight or headlamp
- Personal first-aid basics
- Day pack
- Required uniform and activity clothing
- No-flame alternatives for unit evening activities

Do not pack on the assumption that vehicles can deliver equipment to the campsite. Pack gear so Scouts and leaders can carry it safely from the parking area.

# III. Getting to Camp

Camp Lawton is the first camp on Organization Ridge Road near Mile 20 of the Catalina Highway. If you reach the Palisades Ranger Station, you have gone too far.

The Catalina Highway is paved, wide, and safe when driven attentively, but it is steep and winding.

- Watch carefully for cyclists, wildlife, stopped traffic, and changing weather.
- Obey all posted speed limits and no-passing zones.
- Use pullouts when necessary to allow faster traffic to pass safely.
- Secure loose gear before beginning the climb.
- Check fuel, brakes, tires, coolant, and emergency supplies before departure.
- On the descent, use a lower gear as appropriate rather than continuously riding the brakes.

Cell coverage becomes unreliable on the mountain. Download directions before leaving Tucson.

# IV. Arrival and Check-In

## Arrival Window

Arrive on your session’s designated first day between **1:00 and 3:00 PM**.

- Week 1 arrives Tuesday, June 1.
- Weeks 2 and 3 arrive Sunday.
- Do not arrive early; staff will still be preparing camp.
- Contact the Camp Director in advance when a special arrival time is unavoidable.

## Check-In Procedure

1. **Back into your parking space.** This is required for emergency egress.
2. **Leave unauthorized vehicles in the parking area.** Keep roads and fire lanes clear.
3. **Report to the check-in station** with the unit roster, health forms, accommodation information, and prescription medications.
4. **Complete the medical review.** All medications are turned over to the Camp Health Officer and returned at check-out with the required signature.
5. **Receive your campsite assignment, leader packet, schedule, and issued equipment.**
6. **Meet your Troop Friend.** A staff member will guide the unit through camp, answer routine questions, and serve as a liaison with camp leadership.
7. **Carry equipment to your campsite and set up.**
8. **Attend the safety briefing at 3:00 PM.** All Scouts and leaders must attend.

Your unit will also receive a Fireguard Chart to complete and post in the campsite. It must remain posted even when fires are prohibited.

## Campsite Amenities

Your campsite is your home for the week. Most sites use Adirondack-style sleeping shelters with two raised wooden sleeping platforms.

- Bring a sleeping pad and sleeping bag; cots are generally unnecessary.
- Follow the buddy system and current Scouting America lodging rules.
- Tell camp leadership before arrival when your unit has an odd number of Scouts or needs a lodging accommodation.
- Do not move shelters, dig drainage trenches, drive stakes, or alter the site without approval.
- Use the assigned bear-resistant storage system for food and scented items.
- Keep valuables secured; do not place them in communal food-storage containers.
- Leave the site cleaner than you found it.

# V. Camp Policies

## United States Forest Service Special-Use Permit

Camp Lawton operates on National Forest land under a USFS special-use permit. Permit conditions and posted restrictions take priority over camp preferences.

### Leave No Trace

- Pack out all trash and use designated waste stations.
- Stay on established trails and cleared travel routes.
- Do not cut switchbacks or trample native vegetation.
- Do not collect rocks, plants, feathers, artifacts, or other natural materials.
- Observe wildlife from a distance and never feed it.
- Store all food, trash, toiletries, and scented items as directed.
- Keep group sizes within limits established by camp and the USFS.
- Leave program areas and campsites cleaner than you found them.

| Leave No Trace Principle | What It Means at Camp Lawton |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Plan Ahead and Prepare** | Know activity boundaries, weather conditions, fire restrictions, and unit needs before leaving camp. |
| **Travel on Durable Surfaces** | Stay on established trails and cleared paths. Keep groups together. |
| **Dispose of Waste Properly** | Use designated waste stations and pack out all trash, including food wrappers and craft materials. |
| **Leave What You Find** | Do not collect or move natural, cultural, or historic objects. |
| **Minimize Campfire Impacts** | Use fires only when and where specifically authorized. Maintain a no-flame backup plan. |
| **Respect Wildlife** | Observe from a distance, secure scented items, and report unusual activity. |
| **Be Considerate of Others** | Respect quiet hours, shared facilities, trails, and neighboring campsites. |

### Fire Restrictions

Expect that campsite fires may be prohibited.

- Check the current restriction level with camp staff every day.
- Never start a fire without explicit authorization.
- Use established fire areas only.
- Keep required extinguishing equipment immediately available.
- Extinguish permitted fires completely until cold to the touch.
- Follow staff instructions immediately when conditions change.

### Ground Disturbance

Do not dig, trench, excavate, move rocks, or drive stakes without approval from the Camp Ranger or Camp Director. Historic utility lines may be shallow, and the Forest Service controls physical alterations to the property.

### Waste, Food, and Scented Items

Use designated trash containers and empty unit containers as directed. Store food, trash, toothpaste, deodorant, and other scented items in the assigned secure storage area each night.

Never pour wastewater or food residue onto the ground unless staff has identified an approved disposal method.

### Pets and Service Animals

Pets are not permitted unless specifically approved. Service animals are accommodated under applicable law and camp policy. Notify camp leadership in advance so access, lodging, food storage, and program needs can be coordinated.

## Safeguarding Youth

All participants are responsible for maintaining Scouting America’s youth-safety standards.

- Follow two-deep leadership at all times.
- Never allow one-on-one contact between an adult and a youth who is not their child.
- Use the buddy system for youth movement throughout camp.
- Follow current age, gender, tenting, shower, restroom, and changing-area rules.
- Adults may enter youth privacy areas only for an immediate health or safety emergency or under another specifically authorized procedure.
- Recording devices are prohibited in restrooms, showers, changing areas, and any other place where privacy is expected.
- Report suspected abuse immediately to law enforcement or child-protection authorities as required, then notify the council Scout Executive or Scouts First Helpline at **1-844-SCOUTS1**.
- Report boundary violations, bullying, harassment, threats, or unsafe behavior to camp leadership immediately.
- Do not investigate allegations yourself.

## Health and Medications

A qualified Camp Health Officer is available during the session, with designated backup coverage.

- Bring all prescription medication in original labeled containers.
- Turn medications over during medical check-in.
- Follow the medication-distribution plan established by the Camp Health Officer.
- Retrieve medication at check-out and sign the return record.
- Carry emergency rescue medication only when authorized under the participant’s medical plan and camp procedure.
- Report injuries and illnesses promptly, even when they appear minor.
- Contact your own medical provider before camp regarding ongoing or complex medical concerns.

First-aid supplies are maintained at the Health Lodge and program areas. Trek and off-site activity leaders carry appropriate kits.

## Buddy System

The buddy system is required for Scout movement throughout camp.

- Buddies should remain together while traveling between campsites, program areas, meals, and activities.
- Units should brief Scouts on buddy expectations during arrival.
- Program staff may delay participation until a Scout has an appropriate buddy.
- Leaders remain responsible for knowing where their Scouts are.

## Vehicles

- Unauthorized vehicles remain in the parking area.
- Back into all parking spaces.
- Keep access roads, gates, and fire lanes clear.
- Keep vehicle keys with an adult leader in case evacuation becomes necessary.
- The camp speed limit and access-road speed limit is **10 MPH** unless a lower limit is posted.
- Do not transport youth in truck beds, trailers, or other unapproved locations.

## Personal Firearms, Fireworks, and Range Equipment

Personal firearms, ammunition, fireworks, bows, arrows, air guns, and other range equipment may not be brought to camp without prior written approval from the Camp Director.

Possession or use of unauthorized weapons or pyrotechnics is prohibited.

# VI. Emergencies, Weather, and Wildlife

## Campwide Emergency Signal

The iron bell on the Dining Hall is the primary campwide emergency signal. When it rings continuously:

1. Report immediately to the Parade Grounds at the flagpoles.
2. Do not wait to assemble as a complete unit before moving.
3. Help everyone nearby reach the assembly area.
4. Unit leaders conduct an immediate count and report missing people.
5. Remain quiet and await instructions from camp leadership.
6. Do not leave camp or move vehicles until directed unless an immediate threat requires it.

Emergency assembly information will also be posted in campsites and program areas.

In an evacuation, follow the assigned route and destination. Keep vehicle keys accessible and do not block emergency traffic.

## Active Threat

An active-threat event is unlikely, but leaders should be prepared to act decisively.

- Move away from danger when a safe escape route is available.
- Shelter and secure the area when escape is not safe.
- Silence devices and remain out of sight.
- Call 911 when safely able.
- Follow law-enforcement instructions.
- Physical resistance is a last resort to protect life when no safer option remains.

Camp leadership will provide location-specific instructions during the arrival safety briefing.

## Mountain Weather and Natural Hazards

### Lightning and Summer Storms

Monsoon storms may bring lightning, hail, wind, heavy rain, and rapid temperature changes.

- Range and exposed activities stop at the first sign of unsafe conditions.
- Follow staff shelter instructions immediately.
- Do not shelter beneath isolated trees.
- Keep rain gear and a warm layer available.
- Wait for staff clearance before resuming activities.

### Wildfire

Wildfire is a serious mountain hazard.

- Comply with every fire restriction and evacuation instruction.
- Report smoke, flames, or suspicious fire activity immediately.
- Do not independently investigate a possible fire.
- Keep roads and gates clear for emergency vehicles.

### Terrain and Falling Limbs

Rocky trails, exposed roots, loose gravel, erosion, and falling limbs are persistent hazards.

- Wear closed-toe footwear.
- Carry a flashlight after dark.
- Stay on designated routes.
- Avoid lingering beneath damaged or visibly unstable trees.
- Report hazardous trees, washouts, and trail damage to staff.

## Wildlife

Camp Lawton is wildlife habitat. Enjoy animals from a distance and never approach, feed, corner, or handle them.

Wildlife commonly or occasionally seen in the area includes:

- Squirrels and other small mammals
- Skunks
- Bats
- Turkeys
- Snakes, including rattlesnakes
- Spiders and other arthropods
- Black bears
- Mountain lions
- Numerous mountain bird species

When potentially dangerous wildlife is seen near occupied areas:

1. Keep Scouts back.
2. Do not provoke or pursue the animal.
3. Notify staff immediately.
4. Follow the Camp Ranger’s directions.
5. Secure food, trash, and scented items.

# X. The Tribe of Papago

Before the Order of the Arrow became Scouting’s national honor society, Catalina Council established a local camping recognition tradition known as the Tribe of Papago. The Order of the Arrow later became the nationally recognized program, while the local tradition continued at Camp Lawton as a returning-camper and service recognition system.

The 2027 program recognizes growing commitment to camp through advancement, hiking, service, leadership, and Scout spirit.

## Ranks

### Hunter — First Year

- Earn one merit badge or complete all available requirements in Model Camp
- Hike one approved camp trail
- Demonstrate Scout spirit

### Warrior — Second Year

- Complete one hour of service for Camp Lawton
- Earn two merit badges or complete Model Camp
- Hike one approved camp trail
- Demonstrate Scout spirit

### Medicine Man — Third Year

- Complete two hours of service
- Earn two merit badges
- Provide at least one hour of approved leadership or program assistance
- Hike two approved camp trails
- Demonstrate Scout spirit

### Chief — Fourth Year

- Complete three hours of service
- Earn three merit badges
- Provide approved program assistance and peer leadership
- Hike two approved camp trails
- Demonstrate Scout spirit

### Elder — Adult Leader or Fifth Year

- Encourage Scouts working toward recognition
- Complete three hours of service
- Hike one approved camp trail
- Volunteer in a program area for at least one hour

### Guide — Staff Member

- Serve on staff for more than one week
- Lead or assist with an approved service project
- Teach or directly support Scouts through assigned duties
- Visit assigned campsites consistently
- Learn the purpose and history of the recognition program
- Participate in an approved ceremony
- Receive recommendations from appropriate unit and staff leaders
- Participate in a chapel or unit reflection opportunity
- Lead a unit on an approved camp trail

Unit leaders track progress and submit completed recognition forms. Patches and current requirements are available through the Trading Post or program leadership.

# XI. The Barker Standard

Roy J. Barker was a longtime Scoutmaster, executive-board member, camp staff member, and mentor whose units were known for service and Scout spirit. The Barker Standard recognizes units that contribute meaningfully to the camp community.

Every unit that completes the published requirements may receive recognition, a Tribe of Papago rocker, and a ribbon for the unit flag. Camp staff may also select one especially strong unit for additional recognition.

To earn the Barker Standard, a unit must:

1. Request and pass a uniform inspection by a senior staff member.
2. Request and pass a campsite inspection by a senior staff member.
3. Lead a flag ceremony.
4. Sing a song at a campwide activity.
5. Perform an approved skit, song, or story at the closing campfire.
6. Volunteer for an assigned camp cleanup duty.
7. Complete an approved service project.
8. Consistently demonstrate Scout spirit and live the Scout Oath, Scout Law, motto, slogan, and Outdoor Code.
9. Complete and submit the Barker Standard application.

Requirements may be adjusted in the final leader packet to fit session length and camp needs.

# XV. Frequently Asked Questions

## How many merit badges can a Scout earn?

There are six scheduled merit-badge periods on a standard program day, but registering for six badges is usually too much. Camp is not meant to be a badge factory. Leave room for campwide games, hiking, rest, service, friendships, and the experiences Scouts cannot get at a weekly troop meeting.

Choose a schedule that challenges the Scout without turning the week into an endurance test.

## How large is Camp Lawton?

Camp Lawton is approximately 60 acres. Its relatively compact layout helps Scouts become familiar with the property and staff, but the elevation and steep terrain can make distances feel longer than they appear.

## Why is there no aquatics program?

The former pool became impractical to operate and is no longer available. A future aquatics facility would require substantial funding, construction approval, and Forest Service authorization. Units should not plan for any swimming or boating activity in 2027.

## Why does the range use air rifles rather than .22-caliber rifles?

Camp Lawton operates in a constrained mountain location on National Forest land. Air rifles reduce noise and environmental impact while allowing Scouts to practice core marksmanship skills. The 2027 air-rifle program remains subject to final permit, equipment, and staffing approval.

## What happens if online merit-badge registration goes wrong?

Contact camp before arrival whenever possible. If the issue remains unresolved, bring a clear list of the Scout’s choices and alternatives. Staff will work with the unit at check-in to find the best available solution, but no specific class can be guaranteed after capacity is reached.

## Can our unit arrive early or leave late?

Only by prior arrangement with the Camp Director. Staff need the scheduled turnaround time to prepare safely for each session.

## Can leaders drive gear to the campsite?

Not unless the Camp Director or Ranger specifically authorizes the vehicle. Plan to carry gear from the parking area.

## Will we be allowed to have a campsite fire?

Possibly, but do not count on it. Fire restrictions can change quickly. Bring a complete no-flame plan for unit evenings and cooking.

## What should we do when a Scout is struggling emotionally?

Stay calm, remain present, protect privacy, and involve the appropriate unit and camp leaders. Contact camp leadership promptly when a Scout needs more support than the unit can safely provide. In an immediate safety crisis, notify the Camp Health Officer or Camp Director at once.
